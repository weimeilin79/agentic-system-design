## The green checkmark that lied

Your agent cancels a flight for a customer: right flight, right refund, friendly confirmation, a clean 1.0. Ship it. What the score misses is that it skipped the identity check first. In one study, fifty-eight cases all finished with a perfect result, and in forty-eight of them the agent had broken a rule to get there. The score caught none of it. A traditional program is deterministic, so you check inputs against outputs and you are done. An agent takes a *path* to its answer, and the path can be wrong in ways the answer hides: right state by luck, by a skipped safety step, by two unauthorized calls that happened to cancel out.

So drop the instinct that evaluation is a gate at the end. It is the instrument you steer by, and you build it first, because every pattern in every prior chapter is a change you cannot tell you made without it. Improved the prompt? Added a memory tier? For whom, on what? Without an eval you are decorating.

## Evaluation runs the whole lifecycle

You always evaluate. The real question is *how much*, and it scales with how autonomous the agent is and how costly it is to be wrong: a read-only summarizer needs a light touch, an agent that moves money gets the regulated-system treatment.

"Evaluate the agent" is really five separate evaluations, run at different points in the lifecycle, against different things:

- **The model.** The labs benchmark the base model before you see it; if you fine-tune or distill your own, you evaluate that checkpoint in isolation (instruction-following, tool use, grounding) before it touches an agent.
- **Development.** As you build, evaluation becomes a data problem. You rarely have enough real cases, so you generate synthetic scenarios, task variations, adversarial inputs, edge cases, to iterate before real traffic exists. A word of caution: synthetic data only covers the edge cases you thought to generate, and the ones that bite hardest in production are usually the ones nobody imagined.
- **The evaluator.** The judge, reward model, and rubric are each systems with their own accuracy, so the thing that grades needs its own eval. A pattern below is devoted to it.
- **History.** Replaying against old production data, past incidents, logged transactions, real conversations with known outcomes, is the highest-signal test you have, because it is the real distribution rather than your guess at it. It is also how you compare a new version against the one in production.
- **Production.** Live traffic surfaces the failures no offline set anticipated. This is a stage, a source, and a loop at once: what you catch here becomes tomorrow's test case.

The patterns that follow are the techniques. These five stages are where you apply them.

## The evaluation patterns

**Trajectory Evaluation.** *Score the path the agent took, the tools it called and the reasoning it followed, alongside where it landed.* This is the lesson of the perfect-score-with-a-violation trace, made into a rule. Outcome evaluation asks "did it end in the right state," and you need it, but on its own it hides every dangerous way of arriving there. Trajectory evaluation scores the run itself: did it call the right tools, in a sensible order, with valid parameters, without an unauthorized step or a skipped safety check. The two answer different questions, outcome tells you *whether* it worked, trajectory tells you *how* and *why*, which is the only one that lets you debug. Build both, and weight them by stakes: for a high-consequence agent, a correct outcome reached by a forbidden path counts as a failure.

**Code Graders.** *Grade anything with a checkable answer in code, and save the LLM judge for what genuinely needs reading.* Reaching for an LLM to score everything is slow, expensive, and needlessly fuzzy. A great deal of what an agent does has a checkable answer: did it call `refund` and not `charge`, were the required parameters present, did the final record match the expected state, is the JSON valid. Grade those in code, exactly and for free. Reserve the LLM judge for the things that genuinely need a reader, was the explanation coherent, was the tone right, was the diagnosis sound. Most robust eval suites are mostly deterministic assertions with a thin, expensive layer of judgment on top.

**The Validated Judge.** *An LLM grading an LLM is itself a model that can be wrong, so measure it against humans before you trust it.* The LLM-as-judge is the workhorse of modern evaluation and also its most seductive trap, because it produces confident numbers that feel like ground truth and are not. A judge can be biased toward longer answers, toward its own phrasing, toward the first option in a pair. So treat the judge as a system under test in its own right: write it explicit rubrics rather than "rate this one to ten," and calibrate it against a set of human-labeled examples until its scores actually agree with your experts. Recent work goes further and treats evaluator quality, its calibration, its bias, its agreement with humans, as a first-class thing you measure, because an uncalibrated judge only hides where the human judgment went, it never removes it. A judge you have not validated is a rumor with a number attached.

**Pairwise Comparison.** *Ask the judge which of two runs is better rather than to put an absolute number on one.* Absolute scores drift and mean different things on different days. "Is A better than B" is a question an LLM judge answers far more consistently, which makes pairwise comparison the right tool when you are choosing between a new prompt and the old one, a new model and the incumbent, a candidate and its baseline. Regression testing an agent is mostly this: does the new version beat the version in production on the cases that matter, judged head to head. When you can turn a scoring problem into a comparison, do.

**The Golden Set.** *A small, hand-built set of cases you trust, grown by feeding every production failure back into it.* Quality beats volume here by a wide margin: a hundred carefully constructed cases with known-good trajectories are worth more than a thousand auto-generated ones with vague expected outputs. Build the anchor set by hand, covering the happy paths, the known edge cases, and the failure modes you already fear, and treat each case as a real artifact, task, environment, expected path, gold output. Then make it a living thing. Every time the agent fails in production, extract that interaction, label it, and add it to the set, so the failure can never silently recur. This is the direct payoff of Chapter 8: the overrides you captured are your richest source of golden cases, because they are precisely where a human already told you the agent was wrong.

**Pre-Production Simulation.** *Prove the agent's behavior against replayed and synthetic scenarios in a safe replica before it touches anything real.* An agent that acts in the world cannot take its first reps in production. Run it in a sandbox wired to fake or recorded tools, replay your golden cases and historical incidents through it, and check trajectory and outcome with no possibility of real harm. This is the "treat it like a regulated system" move, non-negotiable for high-stakes agents, and it is what earns the autonomy ladder from Chapter 8: an action graduates from gated to autonomous once it has a clean record here and then in supervised production.

**Eval in CI.** *Wire the suite into the pipeline so nothing ships without passing, exactly like tests.* An eval you run by hand when you remember is an eval you will stop running the week you are busy. The whole point of the golden set is that it runs automatically, on every pull request and every prompt tweak, and blocks the change if a score regresses. Prompts are code (Chapter 5), and code has CI; evaluation is what CI means for an agent. This is the discipline that turns "we think it got better" into "the suite says it got better, here is the diff."

**Production Sampling.** *Grade a slice of live traffic continuously, because offline suites only catch what you already anticipated.* Your golden set only contains failures you already know about, and production is where the unknown ones live, hidden in the drift of real inputs against a world that keeps changing. So evaluate live traffic too, but affordably: run cheap deterministic checks on everything, run the expensive LLM judge on a small sample, five or ten percent, and fold in the direct user signals, the thumbs-down, the escalation, the retried request. Track the scores over time next to latency and cost, and alert when quality drifts down. Then close the loop, every real failure you catch becomes tomorrow's golden case, and the flywheel turns.

## In the wild

Evaluation is the clearest example in the whole book of a pattern becoming platform infrastructure rather than something each team builds alone. Google's ADK ships an evaluation module where you define trajectory tests and rubric-based scoring and run them on every code push, so a regression is caught before it reaches production, the same shape as a unit-test suite, aimed at agent behavior. The other major cloud runtimes treat evaluators as a first-class component alongside memory and identity, and the open-source frameworks give you reusable judges, calibration against human labels, and CI hooks, so you are rarely writing a scoring harness from scratch anymore.

The academic and industry evaluation literature has hardened the practice, too. Two findings drove the 2026 shift away from "LLM-as-judge everywhere." The first is the airline-agent result the chapter opened with: perfect outcome scores hiding procedural violations four times in five, which is the empirical case for grading the trajectory and not just the outcome. The second is a run of studies showing that LLM judges are themselves unreliable, no single judge is best across tasks, and judges carry measurable biases, which is what pushed evaluator calibration from nice-to-have to standard. What settled out is a three-layer consensus: score the outcome, score the trajectory that produced it, and measure the trustworthiness of the judge doing the scoring. The practical advice is now boringly firm: curate a few hundred golden cases before you trust an aggregate number, grow the set from production failures, calibrate every judge against humans, and wire the whole thing into CI.

## Which phase

Evaluation is the odd one out, because it does not sit in a single phase, it wraps around all four. You evaluate the prompt, the context, the harness, and the loop, together, because the thing you are grading is the behavior that emerges from all of them at once. But if it has a home, it is the **loop**, because that is where reliability is won or lost, and evaluation is how you know whether you won. Everything else in this book is a hypothesis about how to build a better agent. Evaluation is the experiment that tells you if you were right, which is why it comes last in this part and underneath everything that follows.
