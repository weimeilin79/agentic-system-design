# Voice & Style Guide
### *Agentic System Design* — writing in Christina Lin's voice

*Derived from "An Old DevRel's Reality Check" (Apr 2026). This is the reference that keeps all 25 chapters sounding like the same person, even drafted out of order over many months. Hand it to any co-author or editor.*

---

## The voice in one sentence

**A veteran practitioner who has the scars, refuses to be dogmatic about any stack, and treats hard-won experience as a bridge to bring the reader in — not a gate to keep them out.**

Humble-confident. Proud of the journey, willing to indict herself. Emotionally direct. Anti-hype without being cynical. The reader should feel *taught by someone who's been burned*, not lectured by an authority.

---

## The arc of a voiced section

Your blog moves in a five-beat arc. Voiced sections of the book (chapter openers, sidebars, closes) should follow the same shape:

1. **The scar** — open from lived experience or a concrete scene ("countless nights in a freezing data center… brain-burning bugs").
2. **The tension** — something threatens the old way of doing things ("But lately, AI has pushed me into a corner.").
3. **The sharp moment** — a specific, almost uncomfortable anecdote that crystallizes it (the CS freshman who couldn't `cd`).
4. **The turn** — a hard self-confronting realization, usually one bold line (**Maybe I'm the one with the problem.**).
5. **The mission** — resolve outward, toward helping the reader ("to share, to help, and to grow together").

Not every section needs all five, but the *movement* — scar → tension → turn → generosity — is the signature. **Reserve the full five-beat arc for the 2–4 chapters that open on a genuine failure or scar**; the rest should rotate through lighter openers (a reframe, a question, the stakes, or a clean straight-in) so the arc keeps its impact and no two adjacent chapters feel the same. See the opener menu in the outline's front matter.

## Sentence mechanics

- **Mix long and short, then cut to a fragment for impact.** Reflective sentences, then a hard stop: "I was speechless." "But lately, AI has pushed me into a corner."
- **One-line paragraphs** at emotional beats. Give the turn its own white space.
- **Rhetorical questions cluster at moments of doubt** — ask two or three in a row, then answer. ("Is this the death of tech as I know it?")
- **Plain words carry the weight.** The essay isn't jargon-dense; the emotion is in ordinary language ("despair," "arrogant," "embarrassing"). Save the jargon for the mechanics sections.
- **Economical.** A 3-minute read that lands. Don't over-explain the feeling — state it and move.

## Formatting devices (use consistently)

- *Italics* → internal monologue and self-talk: *"Why are you even here? Are you wasting my time?"*
- **Bold** → the single turning-point realization per section. One per section, max — bold loses power if it's everywhere.
- Ellipses "…" → a trailing, thinking-out-loud pause. Use sparingly; they're a spice.
- **Short, evocative section headers** — scene- or metaphor-based, not utility labels. Yours: "The Vanishing Barrier," "The 'cd' Moment," "The Mirror," "Back to the Begining." *Not* "Overview" or "Background."
- A **bilingual/cultural touch** where it's genuine (初心 / beginner's mind) — a personal signature, not decoration. Use rarely so it stays meaningful.

## Signature moves (your fingerprints)

- **Antithesis metaphors** — the crisp "X, not Y" that reframes: *a bridge to bring them in, not a gate to keep them out*. This is your strongest rhetorical move; earn one per chapter.
- **Wordplay pairings** — "tech scars and technical debt."
- **The anti-dogma stance** — "technology is transient… no need to be too dogmatic about a specific stack." This is *the* through-line for a vendor-neutral patterns book; it's practically your thesis. Let it echo whenever the vendor debates come up.
- **Vulnerability as credibility** — admit the ugly first reaction, then correct it. It's what makes the authority land.
- **Casual sign-off** — a warm, slightly informal closer, sometimes lowercase, sometimes cheeky.

## Register map — where the voice runs hot vs. cool

The book can't be memoir for 500 pages. Assign register by *function*:

| Section | Register | Voice ratio |
|---|---|---|
| Book intro & close | Full voice | ~80% |
| Part openers | Full voice | ~70% |
| Chapter openers (varies — failure, scene, reframe, question, stakes, or straight-in) | Full voice, except *straight-in* which is clean | ~65% |
| Design Debate sidebars | Full voice | ~65% |
| "When not to use it" reflections | Warm | ~50% |
| Pattern mechanics, sub-patterns | Clean | ~20% |
| Code, tables, Aegis config | Clean/precise | ~10% |

**Rule of thumb:** the moment the reader needs to *do* something (implement, configure, decide fast at 2 a.m.), drop to clean register. The moment you're *framing, persuading, or reflecting*, run the voice hot.

## Do / Don't

**Do**
- Start hard sections from a scene or a scar.
- Steelman both sides of a debate, then admit which mistake *you've* actually made.
- Keep the anti-dogma posture — no stack, no vendor, is sacred.
- Let one bold line carry the turn.

**Don't**
- Force a war story where you don't have a real one — no anecdote beats a fake one.
- Bold or ellipsis more than once or twice per section.
- Let the voice bleed into the mechanics — precision there is a kindness to the reader.
- Slip into vendor-cheerleading (especially Google — the anti-dogma voice is your protection against it).
