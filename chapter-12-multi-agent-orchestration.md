## The org chart lies

A real organization works because people talk to each other. The finance lead pings the compliance lead. Two engineers settle it at a whiteboard. The chart is the formal structure, and the actual work happens in the informal mesh underneath. Build your agents that way, let them all talk to each other, and you get a system that fails in ways you cannot debug, costs many times what it should, and gives different answers on Tuesday than it gave on Monday.

The field learned this in public. In mid-2025 Cognition published an essay titled "Don't Build Multi-Agents," arguing that splitting work across agents fragments context and disperses decisions. Anthropic's engineering guidance said something similar: start with one agent, reach for more only when you must. Meanwhile Google, Microsoft, and AWS were shipping multi-agent as a headline feature. It was the sharpest live disagreement in the field.

Then it resolved, and the answer turned out to be a shape rather than a verdict. Everyone landed on the same one: a single orchestrator owns the context and spawns isolated, short-lived subagents that each do one thing, return a summary, and disappear. The subagents never talk to each other.

Watch five independent teams arrive there within a year of arguing about it. Cognition, the loudest critic, shipped a feature letting its coding agent manage other instances of itself, which is an orchestrator over workers. Anthropic shipped role-scoped subagents that hand their findings back to one owning context. OpenAI turned nested handoff history off by default, so a delegated agent stops inheriting the whole conversation. AutoGen folded into Microsoft's framework with its peer group-chat mode demoted from the flagship. LangChain moved from a supervisor library to supervisor-as-a-tool. The critics were right about the failure and wrong that the answer was one agent. The answer was a specific shape of many.

## The thing that actually breaks

Multi-agent systems fail on cost and latency and debuggability, and those are the symptoms. The disease is context.

Picture a supervisor that routes a financial question to two specialists in parallel, one for finance, one for compliance. Both come back confident. One computed its answer using a definition of revenue that recognizes bookings; the other used one that recognizes cash collected. Neither is wrong inside its own head, and each holds its own memory store with its own accumulated understanding. The supervisor now has two answers that disagree for reasons it cannot see, and no principled way to pick. It will pick anyway.

That is context inconsistency, and it is the structural reason multi-agent orchestration breaks at scale rather than in demos. In a demo the agents share a fresh, small, identical picture of the world. In production they accumulate divergent ones. Definitions drift apart. Business rules that should be shared get held separately. Nothing announces the divergence, because each agent is behaving perfectly according to what it knows.

Everything else follows from this. The reason peer-to-peer topologies cost so much is that keeping agents mutually informed means every wakeup re-reads the full transcript, and the coordination protocol rides in every system prompt, and the number of relationships to maintain grows with the square of the number of agents. The reason multi-agent systems are hard to debug is that the bug lives in the gap between two agents' pictures of the world, which is exactly the place your traces do not look. So the whole design problem reduces to one question: who owns the context, and how little of it can you let diverge?

## The rule: context has one owner

Context has one owner. That part is durable. Who the owner is, and how you enforce it, is a design choice, and right now the trend is decisive: hand it to a single orchestrator.

It won for a plain reason. Something has to consolidate, to merge what came back and decide what happens next, and that is far easier to reason about when exactly one thing does it. A single orchestrator hands you that consolidator for free. Which makes it a convenience rather than a law, and worth holding loosely. The Pipeline below has no orchestrator at all, and an event-driven system coordinating through a shared log has no central agent either, and both satisfy the rule. What none of them do is leave the question of who consolidates unanswered.

That shape looks like this. One agent owns the task and its state. Anything it delegates goes to a subagent that reads the shared store under the scope the orchestrator gives it. That subagent is a real agent, so it runs its own loop and keeps its own scratch as it works, and it can go back and forth with the orchestrator when the task calls for it. Vertical traffic is fine, because the orchestrator owns the truth at both ends of it. What you forbid is horizontal traffic: no sibling channel, no second agent quietly becoming a rival authority on the same fact. When the subagent finishes, it writes its result to the key it owns, the orchestrator folds that into what it knows and decides what happens next, and the subagent's scratch is discarded. The scratch dies; the result survives.

That arrangement gives up something real. Subagents cannot coordinate with each other, discover a sibling's findings mid-flight, or negotiate. In exchange, there is exactly one place the truth lives, one context to debug, and one agent whose picture of the world is authoritative.

Be precise about what that forbids, because it is narrower than it sounds. The enemy is divergent copies. Shared access is fine, and is often the whole point. A shared append-only log that every agent reads, with clear rules about who may write, gives you a single owner too, and event-driven designs built that way satisfy the rule rather than break it. What you cannot have is four agents each growing a private picture and reconciling by talking about it.

Every topology below is a variation on how strictly you hold that line, and the ones that hold it loosely are the ones that bite.

## Agents, tools, and who holds the state

The question underneath "do I need another agent" is almost always "should this be a tool or an agent," and the line between them is state. A tool is stateless: you call `metrics_query(service, window)`, it returns a result, and it remembers nothing, so everything that persists, what has been tried and what was learned, lives in the caller. An agent is stateful: it accumulates a context, keeps a memory, runs a loop, and holds a picture of the world that changes as it works. Promoting a tool to an agent is therefore not a decision about scale. It creates a second place state lives, which is exactly where two definitions of revenue come from. A tool cannot hold a divergent one, because it holds nothing.

That gives you the default: **prefer a tool.** There are three rungs, and you climb only when the work forces you. A **tool** is a stateless call. An **agent as a tool** is a stateless call with a loop inside it: it takes arguments, reasons across several steps, returns a result, and forgets everything, so from the caller's side it is a tool and it belongs in the tool list rather than in the topology. Statelessness is about what survives the call, not about what happens inside it, which is the shape LangChain arrived at when it moved from a supervisor library to supervisor-as-a-tool. A **delegated agent** is the only rung that is a task rather than a call: it has a lifecycle, it can talk with its parent while it works, and it carries context across the flow. Reach for that one only when the work genuinely needs to remember the flow it belongs to.

When you do, the practice worth keeping is **one copy of the state, with the full history.** Each agent will keep its own conversation as it works, because that is what running a loop means, and that is fine. What matters is that one place holds the canonical record: what is known, what was delegated, what came back, and in what order. Agents read from it and write their results back to keys they own, so it is always clear who changed what. The failure is the private, long-lived copy of a shared fact that one agent edits on the side and reconciles later.

This is how the frameworks actually do it. An ADK session is exactly that canonical copy: the ordered event history of everything that happened, plus a `session.state` dictionary of the working facts. A parent hands its invocation context down to its sub-agents, so the whole chain reads and writes that one session while each agent still runs its own conversation; an agent's result lands in a named key, the next agent reads it or has it templated straight into its prompt, and key prefixes scope lifetime and visibility. LangGraph arrives from another direction, with a typed state object flowing through the graph, a reducer merging each node's update, and a checkpointer persisting it. One record, scoped writes, in both. The payoff is replay: state plus ordered history in one place is what lets you re-run a task, resume after a crash, and reconstruct a failure. Without it you have only what each agent happened to hold, in windows that no longer exist, which is why both frameworks tell you to persist the record outside the runtime for anything production.

The protocols split on the same line the rungs do. MCP (Chapter 7) is how an agent talks to tools, and its unit is a call that returns, which is where both a plain tool and an agent-as-a-tool live. A2A is how an agent talks to another agent, and its unit is a task with a lifecycle, because the far end is stateful and may take a while to finish. If you find yourself wanting A2A for something that could have been a call, you are on the wrong rung. A2A now sits under the Linux Foundation with native support across the major clouds, and the property that matters is cross-vendor: your orchestrator and its workers no longer have to come from the same company or even the same framework. It is young enough that the durable move is to build on the primitives every protocol shares, identity, task, message, status, artifact, rather than on the syntax of any one spec.

## When you actually need more than one

Before the topologies, the honest test, because most multi-agent systems exist for bad reasons. It looks like an org chart, it feels tidy, it makes a good diagram. None of that is an engineering reason. Three are.

**The context will not fit.** A task genuinely too large for one window, where subagents each burn their own fresh context on a piece and hand back a summary that fits in the orchestrator's. This is the most common good reason, and it is really a context-management technique wearing a costume.

**A boundary must be enforced.** A billing agent that must not, under any circumstances, hold engineering tools. A subagent handling regulated data that must not see the rest of the conversation. Here the split is a security control, and the isolation is the entire point.

**The work is genuinely parallel.** Several independent queries with no dependency between them, where running them at once is the difference between four seconds and forty. Note the word independent: if the pieces need to know about each other, you are back to context inconsistency, and the parallelism was a mirage.

If your reason is not one of those three, build one agent. Give it better tools (Chapter 7), better context (Chapter 5), a tighter loop (Chapter 10). Every problem you hope to solve by adding an agent, you are more likely to solve by fixing the one you have, and you will not pay the coordination tax to do it.

## The topologies

Five shapes, ordered by how much the agents talk to each other, which is the same as ordering them by how much your context can fragment. Start at the top.

## The Pipeline

*Agent A finishes its work and hands off to agent B, one direction, no return path.*

The loosest coupling there is: a sequence of agents, each doing its stage and passing a structured payload forward. Intake to enrichment to decision to notification. It is the multi-agent version of Chapter 11's Chain, except each stage is a full agent with its own loop rather than a single model call. Nobody talks backward, and nobody negotiates.

**Tradeoffs.** Simple, traceable, and each stage is independently testable and replaceable, which is why this is the shape that survives longest in production. The cost is that a mistake in stage one propagates the length of the pipeline with nothing to catch it, so the handoff payloads need to be strict and validated. **Where the context lives:** in the record that travels with the task. Each stage writes its output to a key it owns and reads the one the previous stage wrote, so ownership moves down the line with the work and no two stages ever claim the same fact. That is why the divergence problem barely touches this shape. **In the wild:** most enterprise document and claims processing, and any workflow that was a business process before anyone said the word agent.

## The Supervisor

*One orchestrator owns the context and spawns isolated, short-lived subagents that each return a summary and end.*

The 2026 production default, and the shape five independent teams converged on. The orchestrator holds the full picture. When it needs something done, it spawns a subagent with a narrow brief, a specific toolset, and a scoped view of the shared state, and that subagent runs its own loop, keeps its own scratch, writes one compressed result back, and dies. The subagents never see each other and never see the whole conversation. One level deep is the norm; supervisors of supervisors is where people get hurt.

**Tradeoffs.** You get specialization, tool isolation, and a way to spend context that would not fit in one window, while keeping one authoritative picture of the truth. The cost is that the orchestrator becomes a bottleneck and a single point of failure, and subagents doing related work cannot help each other, so they sometimes duplicate effort. **Where the context lives:** in one shared store whose schema and keys the orchestrator owns. A subagent reads the scope it is given and keeps its own scratch as it reasons, and that scratch evaporates when it finishes, so the only thing that lands back in shared state is the result it writes, which is why the quality of that summary is the whole ballgame in this shape. **In the wild:** Claude Code's subagents, LangGraph's supervisor, and the coding agents that spawn a fresh instance per file or per test failure.

## Fan-Out

*A supervisor spawns several subagents at once on independent pieces, then aggregates their results deterministically.*

The Supervisor with parallelism, and the only reason to reach for it is latency. The orchestrator decomposes a task into pieces that genuinely do not depend on each other, dispatches them concurrently, and merges the returns. The merge is code, not a model, wherever it can be.

**Tradeoffs.** Real wall-clock gains on independent work, at a token cost that multiplies with the fan and a merge step that has to handle subagents disagreeing or returning nothing. The trap is fanning out work that is not actually independent, at which point you have bought yourself the revenue problem in exchange for two seconds. **Where the context lives:** with the orchestrator, split into disjoint slices, and the word disjoint is load-bearing. If two branches need the same evolving fact, this is the wrong shape. **In the wild:** research agents that read twenty sources at once, and Chapter 11's Parallel and Vote grown up into full agents.

## Debate and Judge

*Several agents produce competing answers or critique each other, and a judge arbitrates.*

Deliberate redundancy for quality. Run the same question through agents with different models, different prompts, or different assigned perspectives, let them argue or critique in a bounded number of rounds, then have a judge pick or synthesize. Microsoft ships a version of this that runs two frontier models in parallel and arbitrates with a third.

**Tradeoffs.** Genuinely better answers on hard, high-stakes questions, at roughly two and a half times the cost of a single model call, which is a premium you should pay deliberately rather than sprinkle on as a default quality booster. A lighter critique variant costs far less. **Where the context lives:** duplicated on purpose, which is the one place independent pictures are the point, since agreement means nothing if the agents started from the same view. The duplication stays safe because it is transient and exactly one judge writes the verdict. **In the wild:** Microsoft's multi-model council pattern, and Chapter 9's evaluator patterns applied at the agent level.

## Peer-to-Peer

*Agents talk to each other directly, ask questions, hand off freely, and wake each other up.*

The org chart, built literally. Every agent can reach every other, a supervisor may mediate but does not own the only context, and the system is expected to coordinate itself. This is the shape that lost the argument, and it is worth knowing exactly why: relationships scale with the square of the agent count, every wakeup re-reads the transcript, the coordination protocol rides in every system prompt, and each agent maintains its own drifting picture of the world with no authority to resolve conflicts. Its frontier extreme is the swarm, hundreds of agents coordinating through learned rather than programmed protocols, which is a genuine research direction and not a thing to put in front of your customers this year.

**Tradeoffs.** Maximum flexibility and emergent behavior, which sounds like a benefit until you have to explain to an auditor why the system did what it did. Cost, latency, and debuggability all degrade together, and the failure mode is silent. **Where the context lives:** everywhere, which is the problem. There is no single authoritative picture, so the revenue disagreement has no principled resolution and simply becomes the answer. **In the wild:** the group-chat frameworks that led the field in 2024 and 2025 and have since been demoted to non-default modes by the vendors that shipped them. If you are reaching for this, be able to say why the Supervisor cannot do it.

## What the shapes have in common

Look back at the five shapes and one thing falls out: each is a different discipline for who writes what. Every one of them has a record. What changes is who is allowed to write to it.

The Supervisor gives the orchestrator ownership of the schema and hands each subagent a scoped view and a key to write its result into. The Pipeline gives each stage one output key that the next stage reads, so writes are owned by position in the chain. Fan-Out assigns disjoint keys, which is what "independent" actually means in practice: no two branches may write the same key, so they cannot contradict each other. Debate lets every participant write its own key and gives exactly one judge the right to write the verdict, which is why duplication is safe there. Peer-to-Peer has no key ownership at all. Everyone reads and writes everything, no agent's version of a fact is authoritative, and the drift has nowhere to be resolved.

So the invariant is not that state is hoarded at the top. It is that every fact in the shared store has exactly one writer, and that writer is known before the run starts. Get that right and agents can read as freely as you like, because there is only ever one version of anything to read.

## In the wild

Google's ADK is the most committed bet in the industry: it treats multi-agent as the default deployment mode rather than an advanced feature, and it uses A2A for cross-agent communication out of the box. The reference architecture is a supervisor talking to workers over A2A, where any worker can also be a non-Google agent, another vendor's, a custom service, a third-party SaaS agent, speaking the same protocol. That is the first major stack designed cross-vendor by default, and it is a real bet that enterprise deployments will mix vendors rather than buy one.

The convergence around it is the more interesting story. Anthropic's guidance still says start with one agent and treat multi-agent as a last resort, and its own multi-agent work uses role-scoped subagents that return to a single owning context. Cognition argued against multi-agent entirely and now ships an orchestrator managing its own instances. OpenAI's SDK made nested handoff history opt-in, which is the same instinct expressed as a default. Microsoft absorbed AutoGen and stopped leading with peer group chat. Everyone arrived at the supervisor with isolated subagents from different directions, some of them while arguing that the whole category was a mistake. When the loudest critic and the most committed vendor build the same shape, that shape is the answer.

## When not to reach for this

The default answer is one agent, and it stays the default longer than you expect. Multi-agent buys you context isolation, enforced boundaries, and parallelism, and it charges you coordination overhead, multiplied token cost, compounded latency, and a debugging story that gets worse with every agent you add. If you cannot name which of the three reasons applies, you do not have a reason. And if you are reaching for peer-to-peer because it models how your team works, remember that your team also has hallway conversations, shared assumptions, and a manager who resolves disputes, and your agents have none of those. They have a context window and whatever you chose to put in it.

## Which phase

This is the **loop** at scale: many loops, and the question of who owns the truth across them. Everything from Part II now applies per agent, since each one has its own context, memory, tools, and gates, and the orchestrator's job is to keep all of that from fragmenting into a system that quietly disagrees with itself. That is why this chapter comes after the split rather than before it. You cannot orchestrate agents you have not yet made reliable, and the fastest way to build an unreliable multi-agent system is to build it out of agents you never got working alone.
